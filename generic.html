End of Module Assignment: Draft

Working Title:"High Tech Croissants"
Proposal:
Lune is a Melbourne, Australia headquartered croissanterie chain. It’s described as the home of the world’s best croissant and reportedly a primary reason some tourists visit Melbourne (Strand, 2016). In 2012, the original Lune location was fashioned in a large brick warehouse with a central climate-controlled glass enclosed workspace at its centre. The ideal place for creating seemingly ethereal pastry products. Lune’s founder Kate Reid, an aerospace engineer, knows the significance of precision in working practices & habits, productivity and management efficiencies. Especially relevant now with 5 locations in 2 major Australian cities, Lune’s focus has arguably become expansion. America/Europe expansion is a company goal (Strand, 2016).
Although, expansion to new locations in the hospitality industry is problematic. It involves immense risk. This risk is primarily characterised by a failure to capture the market. Furthermore, it’s notably difficult for a business to predict a new market’s behaviour and how to implement themselves when said business is familiar with capturing a market that is local not global; the case for a croissanterie (Strand, 2016). Therefore, this risk must be mitigated or at the very least it must be risk that is understood/analysable, expected or predicted in order to best ensure security of the business. Any predictive solutions also have multifaceted applications to business operations in current locations (Busch, 2022).
Currently, Lune utilises a variety of emerging trends in computer science to propel their business operations. These include, social media, big data and cloud computing (Brookshear & Brylow, 2019). Specifically, a minimalistic and artistic social media presence built Lune’s sizeable and enthralled customer base. Furthermore, Lune’s affiliation with Lightspeed evidences just one use of big data and cloud computing. Lightspeed is a cloud-based POS (point-of-sale) and e-commerce software provider that supplies Lune with POS ‘Insight’, ‘Purchase’ and ‘Produce’ software in the context of product/inventory management (Lightspeed, no date). Lune is also cashless so the business relies on big data technologies even more so and expectedly, email services and other simple big data technologies are used (Wright, 2018).
Unfortunately, exploration of the technologies utilised has revealed a failure by Lune to employ artificial intelligence (AI) forecasting technology. This is a problem because AI is becoming the cutting-edge difference that advantages one business over another and is the solution to mitigating risk associated with expansion, informing innovation and bolstering the business as a whole (Mohapatra, 2019). Forecasting AI (FAI) would allow Lune to predict and adapt to customer preferences over time (individualisation), to determine optimal locations and make revenue projections for potential expansions based on variables (e.g. income levels, traffic, competitor presence, etc.) and inform future product development dependent on customer data (Antwi & Al-Dherasi, 2019; Hyperight, 2021; Nelson, 2022). Going even further, FAI related to the Internet of Big Things (IoT) would make predictions regarding the machinery utilised in context of their tuning, maintenance and support (Hyperight, 2021).
FAI as a solution is ideal to provide Lune with a competitive advantage. Their unicorn-esque success thus far puts them in an unusual position in which they have the capital to invest in FAI technology unlike other direct competitors (Mohapatra, 2019). Automation and optimisation produced by cognitive technologies met with Lune’s current business processes could promise outstanding growth (Antwi & Al-Dherasi, 2019).
